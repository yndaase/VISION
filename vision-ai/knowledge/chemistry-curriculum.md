# Chemistry Curriculum Knowledge Base - WASSCE Preparation
## Ghana SHS Year 2 Chemistry

## Overview
This knowledge base covers key chemistry topics for WASSCE (West African Senior School Certificate Examination) preparation, based on the Ghana Ministry of Education SHS Year 2 Chemistry curriculum.

---

## Section 1: Energy Changes (Physical Chemistry - Thermochemistry)

### Key Concepts:
- **Enthalpy (H)**: H = U + PV — thermodynamic quantity describing energy of a system
- **Enthalpy Change (ΔH)**: ΔH = H(products) − H(reactants)
- **Exothermic reactions**: Release heat; ΔH is negative; products have less energy than reactants
- **Endothermic reactions**: Absorb heat; ΔH is positive; products have more energy than reactants
- **Standard conditions**: 298 K (25°C), 101.3 kPa (1 atm), 1.0 mol/dm³ for solutions

### Types of Standard Enthalpy Changes:
1. **ΔH°rxn** — Standard enthalpy of reaction (depends on stoichiometry)
2. **ΔH°f** — Standard enthalpy of formation (forming 1 mole of compound from elements; elements in standard state = 0)
3. **ΔH°c** — Standard enthalpy of combustion (complete combustion of 1 mole in O₂)
4. **ΔH°n** — Standard enthalpy of neutralisation (acid + alkali → 1 mole water; strong acid/base ≈ −57 kJ/mol)
5. **ΔH°soln** — Standard enthalpy of solution (dissolving 1 mole ionic substance in excess water)
6. **ΔH°hyd** — Standard enthalpy of hydration (1 mole gaseous ions dissolved in water)

### Key Formula:
- ΔH°rxn = Σn·ΔH°f(products) − Σm·ΔH°f(reactants)

### Hess's Law:
- Total enthalpy change is independent of reaction pathway
- ΔH₃ = ΔH₁ + ΔH₂ (if reaction occurs in steps)
- Rules: reverse equation → change sign; multiply equation → multiply ΔH

### Born-Haber Cycles:
- Energy cycles for ionic compounds using Hess's Law
- Used to calculate lattice energy
- Steps: sublimation, atomisation, ionisation, electron affinity, lattice energy

### Bond Enthalpy:
- Energy to break 1 mole of a specific covalent bond in gaseous molecules
- ΔH°rxn = Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed)
- Bond breaking = endothermic; Bond forming = exothermic

### Experimental Determination:
- Q = m × c × ΔT (where c = 4.18 J/g°C for water)
- Heat of combustion: burn substance under water, measure temperature rise
- Heat of neutralisation: mix acid and base, measure temperature change
- Heat of solution: dissolve solid in water, measure temperature change

## Section 2: Chemical Kinetics

### Key Concepts:
- **Rate of reaction**: Change in concentration per unit time
- Rate = −(1/a) × Δ[A]/Δt = (1/c) × Δ[C]/Δt
- **Initial rate**: Rate at t = 0
- **Average rate**: Change in concentration over a time interval
- **Instantaneous rate**: Rate at a specific moment (slope of tangent on concentration-time graph)

### Factors Affecting Rate:
1. **Temperature**: Higher temp → more kinetic energy → more effective collisions
2. **Concentration**: Higher concentration → more particles → more collisions
3. **Surface area**: Larger surface area → more contact → faster reaction
4. **Catalyst**: Provides alternative pathway with lower activation energy
5. **Pressure** (gases): Higher pressure → particles closer → more collisions

### Collision Theory:
- Particles must collide with correct orientation AND sufficient energy (≥ activation energy)
- Effective collision = collision that leads to reaction
- Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curve shows energy distribution of particles
- At higher temperature: curve shifts right, more particles exceed activation energy

### Rate Equations:
- Rate = k[A]ˣ[B]ʸ
- x, y = orders with respect to each reactant (determined experimentally)
- Overall order = x + y
- k = rate constant

### Orders of Reaction:
- **Zero order**: Rate = k (independent of concentration)
- **First order**: Rate = k[A] (doubling [A] doubles rate)
- **Second order**: Rate = k[A]² (doubling [A] quadruples rate)
- **Half-life (first order)**: t½ = 0.693/k
- **Rate-determining step**: Slowest step in reaction mechanism

## Section 3: Dynamic Equilibrium

### Key Concepts:
- **Reversible reaction**: Products can reform reactants (shown with ⇌)
- **Irreversible reaction**: Goes in one direction only (shown with →)
- **Dynamic equilibrium**: Forward and reverse rates are equal; concentrations remain constant in a closed system

### Equilibrium Constants:
- **Kc**: Ratio of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium
  - For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ
- **Kp**: Uses partial pressures (for gases)
  - Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn where Δn = moles of gaseous products − moles of gaseous reactants
- **Ksp**: Solubility product for sparingly soluble salts
  - For AxBy ⇌ xAʸ⁺ + yBˣ⁻: Ksp = [Aʸ⁺]ˣ[Bˣ⁻]ʸ

### Significance of K values:
- K >> 1: Reaction favours products
- K << 1: Reaction favours reactants
- K = 1: Roughly equal concentrations

### Le Chatelier's Principle:
- If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to oppose the change
- **Concentration**: Add reactant → shifts right; remove product → shifts right
- **Pressure**: Increase pressure → shifts to side with fewer gas moles
- **Temperature**: Increase temp → shifts in endothermic direction (K changes)
- **Catalyst**: Speeds up both directions equally; does NOT shift equilibrium

### Industrial Applications:
- **Haber Process** (NH₃): N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, ΔH = −92 kJ/mol
  - Conditions: ~450°C, ~200 atm, iron catalyst
- **Contact Process** (H₂SO₄): 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃, ΔH = −196 kJ/mol
  - Conditions: ~450°C, ~1-2 atm, V₂O₅ catalyst

## Section 4: Acids, Bases and Salts

### Acid-Base Theories:
1. **Arrhenius**: Acid produces H⁺ in water; Base produces OH⁻ in water
2. **Brønsted-Lowry**: Acid donates proton (H⁺); Base accepts proton
   - Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one H⁺
   - Amphiprotic substances can act as both (e.g., water)
3. **Lewis**: Acid accepts electron pair; Base donates electron pair

### Physical Properties:
- Acids: sour taste, turn blue litmus red, pH < 7, conduct electricity
- Bases: bitter taste, slippery feel, turn red litmus blue, pH > 7

### Chemical Properties of Acids:
- Acid + metal → salt + H₂ gas
- Acid + carbonate → salt + H₂O + CO₂
- Acid + base → salt + water (neutralisation)

### Chemical Properties of Bases:
- Base + acid → salt + water
- Base + ammonium salt → salt + water + NH₃
- Base + heavy metal salt → metal hydroxide precipitate

### Strong vs Weak Acids/Bases:
- **Strong acids** (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃): fully dissociate, low pH, high conductivity
- **Weak acids** (CH₃COOH): partially dissociate, higher pH, lower conductivity
- Strong acid + strong base neutralisation: ΔH ≈ −57 kJ/mol

### Salts:
- Normal salt: all H⁺ replaced (NaCl)
- Acidic salt: partial replacement (NaHSO₄)
- Basic salt: partial OH⁻ replacement
- Double salt: two cations (e.g., potash alum)
- Complex salt: central metal with ligands

### Acid-Base Titration:
- **Titrant**: known concentration (in burette)
- **Analyte**: unknown concentration (in flask)
- **Endpoint**: indicator colour change
- **Equivalence point**: stoichiometric amounts have reacted
- Average titre = average of consistent readings (within ±0.20 cm³)
- Calculations: n = CV; use mole ratios from balanced equation

### Indicators and pH ranges:
- Methyl orange: 3.1–4.4 (red to orange)
- Litmus: 5.0–8.0 (red to blue)
- Phenolphthalein: 8.3–10.0 (colourless to pink)

### Types of Titration:
- **Simple titration**: direct acid-base reaction
- **Back titration**: excess reagent added, then titrated
- **Double-indicator titration**: two indicators for mixtures (e.g., Na₂CO₃/NaHCO₃)

## Section 5: Periodic Table Trends (Period 3)

### Period 3 Elements: Na, Mg, Al, Si, P, S, Cl, Ar

### Physical Trends Across Period 3:
- **Metallic character**: decreases left to right
- **Melting/boiling points**: increase Na→Si (metallic/covalent bonds), decrease Si→Ar (molecular)
- **Density**: generally increases Na→Al, then varies
- **Electrical conductivity**: Na, Mg, Al (good conductors); Si (semiconductor); P, S, Cl, Ar (insulators)

### Chemical Reactions:
- Na + H₂O → NaOH + H₂ (vigorous)
- Mg + H₂O → Mg(OH)₂ + H₂ (slow with cold water)
- Reactions with O₂: form oxides (Na₂O, MgO, Al₂O₃, SiO₂, P₄O₁₀, SO₂)
- Reactions with Cl₂: form chlorides (NaCl, MgCl₂, AlCl₃, SiCl₄, PCl₃)

### Compounds of Period 3:
- **Hydrides**: ionic (NaH, MgH₂) → covalent (SiH₄, PH₃, H₂S, HCl)
- **Oxides**: basic (Na₂O, MgO) → amphoteric (Al₂O₃) → acidic (SiO₂, P₄O₁₀, SO₃)
- **Hydroxides**: NaOH (strong base) → Al(OH)₃ (amphoteric) → H₂SO₄ (strong acid)
- **Chlorides**: ionic/neutral (NaCl, MgCl₂) → covalent/acidic (AlCl₃, SiCl₄, PCl₅)

## Section 6: Halogens (Group 17)

### Physical Properties (F, Cl, Br, I, At):
- Physical state: gas → gas → liquid → solid (down the group)
- Colour: pale yellow → yellow-green → reddish-brown → purple-black
- Melting/boiling points: increase down group (stronger van der Waals forces)
- Bond energy: decreases down group (F has anomalously low bond energy due to lone pair repulsion)

### Chemical Properties:
- React with metals → ionic halides
- React with hydrogen → hydrogen halides (HF, HCl, HBr, HI)
- **Displacement reactions**: more reactive halogen displaces less reactive halide
  - Reactivity: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂
- **Precipitation reactions** with AgNO₃:
  - Cl⁻ → white AgCl precipitate
  - Br⁻ → pale yellow AgBr precipitate
  - I⁻ → yellow AgI precipitate
- **Oxidising strength**: decreases down group (F₂ strongest)

### Halide Reactions with Concentrated H₂SO₄:
- NaF/NaCl: form HF/HCl only (weak reducing agents)
- NaBr: forms HBr + SO₂ (moderate reducing agent)
- NaI: forms HI + SO₂ + S + H₂S (strongest reducing agent)

### Acid Strength of Hydrogen Halides:
- Order: HF << HCl < HBr < HI
- HF is weak acid (strong H-F bond, 565 kJ/mol)
- HCl, HBr, HI are strong acids (weaker bonds, easier dissociation)
- Bond strength decreases down group → acid strength increases

### Uses of Halogens:
- Chlorine: water purification, bleaching, disinfectants
- Iodine: antiseptics
- Halogenated hydrocarbons: solvents, refrigerants (CFCs now banned)

## Section 7: Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure

### Electronegativity:
- Pauling scale: 0.7 (Cs) to 4.0 (F)
- Increases across period, decreases down group
- Electronegativity difference determines bond type:
  - < 0.5: non-polar covalent
  - 0.5–1.7: polar covalent
  - > 1.7: ionic

### VSEPR Theory:
- Electron pairs repel each other → arrange to minimise repulsion
- Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs
- Molecular shapes:
  - 4 bonding pairs: tetrahedral (109.5°) — CH₄
  - 3 bonding pairs: trigonal planar (120°) — BF₃
  - 2 bonding pairs: linear (180°) — CO₂
  - 3 bonding + 1 lone pair: trigonal pyramidal (107°) — NH₃
  - 2 bonding + 2 lone pairs: bent (104.5°) — H₂O

### Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds:
- **Sigma bond**: head-on overlap of orbitals; found in all single bonds; allows free rotation
- **Pi bond**: sideways overlap of p-orbitals; found in double/triple bonds; restricts rotation
- Single bond = 1σ; Double bond = 1σ + 1π; Triple bond = 1σ + 2π

### Hybridisation:
- **sp³**: 4 sigma bonds, tetrahedral (109.5°) — alkanes (CH₄, C₂H₆)
- **sp²**: 3 sigma + 1 pi bond, trigonal planar (120°) — alkenes (C₂H₄), benzene
- **sp**: 2 sigma + 2 pi bonds, linear (180°) — alkynes (C₂H₂)

## Section 8: Organic Chemistry

### Alkanes (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂):
- Saturated hydrocarbons; single bonds only
- IUPAC naming: longest chain = parent; substituents numbered from nearest end
- Prefixes: meth(1), eth(2), prop(3), but(4), pent(5), hex(6), hept(7), oct(8), non(9), dec(10)
- **Reactions**: combustion (complete → CO₂ + H₂O; incomplete → CO/C), halogenation (free radical, UV light), cracking, reforming
- **Physical properties**: boiling point increases with chain length; branching decreases boiling point; insoluble in water

### Alkenes (CₙH₂ₙ):
- Unsaturated; contain C=C double bond
- **Reactions** (addition reactions):
  - + H₂ (Ni catalyst, heat) → alkane (hydrogenation)
  - + Br₂ → dibromoalkane (decolourises bromine water — test for unsaturation)
  - + H₂O (H₃PO₄ catalyst) → alkanol (hydration)
  - + HX → haloalkane (Markovnikov's rule: H adds to carbon with more H)
  - + KMnO₄ → diol (purple → brown)
- **Isomerism**: structural (position, chain) and geometric (cis/trans)

### Alkynes (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂):
- Contain C≡C triple bond; sp hybridised; linear (180°)
- **Reactions**: similar to alkenes but can add twice
  - Hydration → aldehyde (ethyne) or ketone (other alkynes)
  - Terminal alkynes are acidic (react with NaNH₂)
- Preparation: from dihalides + strong base; from calcium carbide + water

### Benzene (C₆H₆):
- Aromatic compound; planar hexagonal ring; sp² hybridised carbons
- Delocalised π electrons give extra stability (aromatic stabilisation)
- Prefers **substitution** reactions (not addition — would disrupt aromaticity)
- **Reactions**:
  - Halogenation: + Cl₂/Br₂ (AlCl₃ catalyst) → halobenzene + HCl
  - Nitration: + HNO₃/H₂SO₄ (25-60°C) → nitrobenzene
  - Friedel-Crafts alkylation: + RCl (AlCl₃) → alkylbenzene
  - Friedel-Crafts acylation: + RCOCl (AlCl₃) → acylbenzene
  - Hydrogenation: + 3H₂ (Ni, pressure) → cyclohexane

### Alkanols (CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH):
- Contain -OH group; general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH
- **Classification**: primary (1°), secondary (2°), tertiary (3°) based on carbon bearing -OH
- **Physical properties**: high boiling points (hydrogen bonding); solubility decreases with chain length
- **Reactions**:
  - Combustion → CO₂ + H₂O
  - Oxidation: 1° → aldehyde → carboxylic acid; 2° → ketone; 3° → no reaction
  - Esterification: + carboxylic acid (H₂SO₄ catalyst) → ester + water
  - Reaction with Na → alkoxide + H₂
- **Tests**: Lucas test (1°/2°/3°); Iodoform test (ethanol and methyl ketones → CHI₃)
- **Preparation**: hydration of alkenes; fermentation (C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂)

### Alkanoic Acids (CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH):
- Contain -COOH (carboxyl group); weak acids
- General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH (n ≥ 0)
- **Physical properties**: high boiling points (hydrogen bonding/dimerisation); solubility decreases with chain length
- **Reactions**:
  - + NaOH → sodium alkanoate + water
  - + NaHCO₃ → salt + H₂O + CO₂ (effervescence — distinguishes from alkanols)
  - + alkanol (H₂SO₄) → ester + water (esterification)
  - + Mg → salt + H₂
- **Tests**: blue litmus → red; effervescence with NaHCO₃; fruity smell with ethanol (ester test)
- **Preparation**: oxidation of primary alcohols or aldehydes; hydrolysis of nitriles or esters

## Key Organic Reaction Types:
- **Substitution**: one atom/group replaced by another (alkanes + halogens; benzene reactions)
- **Addition**: atoms added across double/triple bond (alkenes, alkynes)
- **Elimination**: atoms removed to form double bond (dehydration of alcohols)
- **Oxidation**: gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen
- **Esterification**: acid + alcohol → ester + water (reversible)

## Important Reagents and Conditions:
- Bromine water: test for unsaturation (decolourises with alkenes/alkynes)
- KMnO₄: oxidising agent (purple → brown/colourless)
- Concentrated H₂SO₄: catalyst for esterification, dehydration
- AlCl₃/FeBr₃: catalyst for Friedel-Crafts and halogenation of benzene
- Ni/Pt/Pd: hydrogenation catalyst

## WASSCE Chemistry Tips:
1. Always balance equations before calculating
2. Show units in all calculations
3. Know the difference between strong and weak acids/bases
4. Understand Le Chatelier's principle for equilibrium questions
5. Learn IUPAC naming rules thoroughly
6. Practice enthalpy calculations using Hess's Law
7. Know the tests for functional groups (alkenes, alkanols, alkanoic acids)
8. Understand trends in the periodic table (Period 3 and Group 17)
